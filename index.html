Introduction to Sampling Theory

General Mathematics 11

Fourth Quarter | S.Y. 2025-2026

Introduction

In research, it is often impossible to collect data from every single person in a specific group due to time, money, and effort. This is where Sampling comes in.

This module will guide you through:

  • The fundamental concepts of sampling.
  • The different techniques used to select respondents.
  • How to mathematically determine how many people you actually need for your study.

Lesson 1: Fundamental Concepts

Before we learn "how" to sample, we must understand "who" we are sampling and "what" we are measuring.

1. Population vs. Sample

Term Definition Example
Population Refers to the totality (entire group) of observations or elements from a set of data. All Grade 11 students enrolled in your school for the current school year.
Real-world: Government Census.
Sample A subset (smaller group) of the entire population. A group of 50 students selected to answer a survey.
Real-world: A few milliliters of blood for a test.

1. Fundamental Concepts (Cont.)

2. Parameter vs. Statistic

These terms tell us if a number comes from the whole group or just the small group.

Parameter: A numerical measure that describes a characteristic of a Population.

Memory Aid: Parameter goes with Population.

Example: The average daily allowance of all students in the Philippines.

Statistic: A numerical measure that describes a characteristic of a Sample.

Memory Aid: Statistic goes with Sample.

Example: The average daily allowance of 100 randomly selected students.

Relationship: We calculate the Statistic to estimate the Parameter.

Lesson 2: Sampling Techniques

How do we choose who gets to be part of the sample? We use specific techniques divided into two categories: Probability and Non-Probability.

Part A: Probability Sampling

Definition: A technique where each member of the population has a known probability (chance) of being selected. This removes bias.

1. Simple Random Sampling

Every member of the population has an equal chance of being selected. This is the most basic technique.

How to do it:

  • Lottery/Fishbowl Method: Write names on paper, put them in a container, shake, and pick.
  • Random Number Generators: Use apps (like "=RAND()" in Excel) or a Table of Random Numbers.

2. Systematic Random Sampling

This method selects every "$k$-th" element from a list of the population. It is more organized than simple random sampling.

Step-by-Step Procedure:

  1. Identify Total Population ($N$) and Sample Size needed ($n$).
  2. Calculate Interval ($k$) using: $k = N / n$ (Round to nearest whole number).
  3. Randomly select a starting number between 1 and $k$.
  4. Select every $k$-th person after that start.

Example:

Population: 250, Sample Needed: 71.

1. Calculate $k$: $250 / 71 = 3.52$. Round to 4.

2. Random Start: Pick a number from 1 to 4 (e.g., 2).

3. Selection: Student #2, #6 (2+4), #10 (6+4)... keep adding 4 until you have 71 students.

3. Stratified Random Sampling

Used when the population is divided into subgroups called strata (e.g., Grade Level). Ensures fair representation.

Procedure: Calculate % for each group: $(\text{Group} / \text{Total}) \times \text{Sample}$.

Example: Sample = 200, Total Pop = 6,000.

Strata (Grade) Population Percentage (Pop / 6000) Calculation (Sample x %) Final Sample
Grade 7 1,200 20% (0.20) $200 \times 0.20 = 40$ 40
Grade 8 1,100 18.3% (0.183) $200 \times 0.183 = 36.6$ 37
Grade 9 1,000 16.7% (0.167) $200 \times 0.167 = 33.3$ 33
Grade 10 900 15% (0.15) $200 \times 0.15 = 30$ 30
Grade 11 900 15% (0.15) $200 \times 0.15 = 30$ 30
Grade 12 900 15% (0.15) $200 \times 0.15 = 30$ 30
TOTAL 6,000 100% 200

4. Cluster (Area) Sampling

Used when the population is too big or spread out geographically (e.g., by Region, Province, District).

How it works:

  1. Divide population into clusters (groups).
  2. Randomly select a few clusters.
  3. Survey everyone inside those selected clusters.

Key Difference:

  • Stratified: You take a few people from every group.
  • Cluster: You take everyone from a few groups.

Part B: Non-Probability Sampling

Definition: There is bias in the selection. Not everyone has a chance to be selected. Often done for convenience.

  • Convenience Sampling: Selecting respondents based on ease of access.
    Ex: Asking classmates in the same room.
  • Purposive (Judgmental) Sampling: Choosing respondents based on specific criteria/expertise.
    Ex: Selecting only students who play online games for a gaming study.
  • Snowball (Chain-Referral) Sampling: Used when participants are hard to find. Find one, ask them to refer a friend.
    Ex: Rare hobby study.
  • Quota Sampling: Setting a target number for subgroups but selecting them non-randomly.
    Ex: "I need 10 boys," then interviewing the first 10 boys you see.

Lesson 3: Determining Sample Size

Researchers use formulas to find the "sweet spot"—a number small enough to manage, but large enough to be accurate.

1. Slovin’s Formula

Use when you know the Total Population size and you are conducting a descriptive study.

$$ n = \frac{N}{1 + Ne^2} $$

Where: $n$ = Sample Size, $N$ = Total Population, $e$ = Margin of Error.

Example: Pop = 1,000, Error = 5% (0.05)

1. Square error ($e^2$): $0.05 \times 0.05 = 0.0025$

2. Multiply $N \times e^2$: $1,000 \times 0.0025 = 2.5$

3. Add 1: $1 + 2.5 = 3.5$

4. Divide $N$: $1,000 / 3.5 = 285.71 \rightarrow$ 286 students.

2. Green’s Rule of Thumb

Use specifically for Regression Analysis (predicting variables). Slovin's formula is not used here.

$$ n \ge 50 + 8k $$

Where: $n$ = Minimum Sample Size, $k$ = Number of Independent Variables (Predictors).

Example:

Predict Math Score based on (1) Study Hours and (2) Attendance.

$k = 2$.

$n \ge 50 + 8(2) \rightarrow n \ge 50 + 16 \rightarrow$ 66 participants.

Exercises: Part 2

Solve the following. Show your solution steps.

Problem A: Population of 1,500 students. 5% margin of error. Using Slovin's Formula, find the sample size.

Given: $N = 1500$, $e = 0.05$.

$$ n = \frac{1500}{1 + 1500(0.05)^2} $$

$$ n = \frac{1500}{1 + 1500(0.0025)} $$

$$ n = \frac{1500}{1 + 3.75} = \frac{1500}{4.75} $$

$$ n = 315.78 \rightarrow \mathbf{316 \text{ students}} $$

Problem B: Predict "Life Expectancy" based on 3 independent variables: Diet, Exercise, and Genetics. Using Green's Rule, what is the minimum sample size?

Given: $k = 3$.

$$ n \ge 50 + 8(3) $$

$$ n \ge 50 + 24 $$

$$ \mathbf{74 \text{ participants}} $$
